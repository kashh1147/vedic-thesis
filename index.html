Brief

An institution for a language without a home.

Sanskrit is the source of most modern Indian languages and a vessel of thousands of years of literature, philosophy, and ritual. Yet India has no purpose-built campus dedicated to its research. This thesis proposes one — a 49,000 sqm campus in Ahmedabad where scholars, students, and the public can engage with a living tradition.

The brief required seven distinct programs to coexist on a single site: a research centre, a Vedic pathshala, an institute, residential quarters, an auditorium and museum, a goshala, and a temple. The challenge was not to fit them — it was to find an organizing logic that gave the campus coherence without flattening the differences between them.

The plan does not borrow from tradition. It is structured by it.

That logic came from two sources: the Vastu Purusha mandala — the cosmological grid that has organized Indian buildings for two millennia — and the gurukul typology, where teacher and student live and learn around a shared courtyard. Neither was applied as ornament. Both shaped the geometry of the site, the orientation of the buildings, and the path a visitor takes from the entrance to the central temple.

Concept

Five elements, one campus.

Vastu Purusha holds that every built space is animated by five elemental forces — pancha bhuta. The campus assigns each zone to one element, and each element shapes the architectural character of its zone: water bodies in the public realm, courtyards of earth in the residential clusters, fire in the yagyashala, air through the open pathshalas, and the void of space at the symbolic centre.

Site

Between the city, the airport, and the green belt.

The site sits in north-east Ahmedabad along the Hansol road, framed by a 32-metre-wide approach road on three sides and a thick vegetation buffer on the fourth. AUDA classifies it as a knowledge and education zone. Its scale (49,000 sqm) and the fact that it is bounded by roads on three sides made symmetrical, axial planning viable in a way few urban sites in Ahmedabad allow.

Primary Axis

Vastu prescribes that ceremonial buildings face north. The site rotates the entire grid to a north-south orientation, placing the Shiva temple at the geometric and symbolic centre. Every other building references the temple through axial sightlines.

Process

From an empty site to a campus, in five moves.

Establish the access

The 49,000 sqm site is bounded by a 40-metre approach road on the north and east. The main entry sits on the central north axis; a secondary entry on the east. Both align to the campus's primary north-south spine.

Lay the Vastu grid

The site is divided into a square grid oriented to the cardinal directions, in keeping with the Vastu Purusha mandala. The grid becomes the substrate for all subsequent placement.

Carve the volumes

Volumes are extruded from the grid and pulled apart to create courtyards. Solid and void alternate. The result is a campus that feels porous from the ground but reads as composed from above.

Anchor the centre

The Shiva temple is placed at the geometric centre of the site. All major buildings — research centre, pathshala, residential, auditorium — orient themselves toward it. The campus reads as a mandala from the air.

Layer the landscape

Water bodies trace the central north-south axis, descending from the temple as a representation of the Ganga. A green belt encircles the perimeter, buffering noise from the airport and creating a microclimate two to three degrees cooler than the surrounding city.

Reflection

What this thesis taught me.

Designing a campus for Sanskrit forced a question I hadn't expected: how do you make a building for a language? The answer wasn't in the program — it was in the planning. Vastu Purusha and the gurukul gave me a way to organize 49,000 sqm without resorting to either pastiche or generic institutional planning.

The thesis also taught me how complex real campus projects are. Seven different programs, three user types, religious and secular spaces side-by-side, vehicular and pedestrian flows that cannot cross — every move has consequences three steps downstream. That kind of multi-stakeholder, multi-program coordination is what drew me to construction project management afterward, and it is the framework I bring to the work I do now.

If I returned to this project today, I would push further on passive cooling strategies and tighten the residential phasing. But the central organizing idea — that a contemporary institution can be structured by an ancient logic without becoming a museum of it — is one I still believe in.

Kashyap Joshi
